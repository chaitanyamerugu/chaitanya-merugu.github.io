Artifact Value Proposition

This framework gives AI/ML professionals and students a systematic, repeatable method for evaluating which work best represents their competencies — and then adapting how that work is presented to maximize relevance for recruiters, enterprise architects, faculty, and organizations with distinct needs and decision criteria.

Objective

To critically evaluate a body of AI/ML work and apply a structured selection framework — identifying which artifacts best represent real professional competencies — then systematically adapt the presentation of each artifact for four distinct professional audiences with different decision-making priorities.

Workshop 4 Learning Outcomes Demonstrated
  • Critically evaluate and select artifacts that best represent AI/ML competencies — using a scored, multi-criteria evaluation matrix applied across all portfolio candidates
  • Customize portfolio artifacts for different audiences — producing four audience-specific adaptations of the same core work, each tailored to what that audience actually needs to see
Introduction — Why Curation and Adaptation Matter

In a competitive AI/ML landscape, having strong work is necessary but not sufficient. The ability to critically evaluate what to show and how to show it is itself a professional competency — one that distinguishes candidates who understand their audience from those who simply dump everything they've ever built into a folder.

As someone with 5+ years of enterprise SAP experience now expanding into AI/ML, I face a specific curation challenge: I have deep technical work across two domains — SAP cloud engineering and emerging AI/ML — and my audiences have radically different literacy levels and success criteria for each.

This artifact documents the systematic process I used to select, prioritize, and adapt my AIML-500 portfolio work — making the curation decision itself a demonstration of the competency it produces.

Step 1: Evaluation Criteria Framework

Before selecting artifacts, I established five weighted criteria to evaluate each candidate piece objectively. This prevents selection bias toward work that "feels impressive" rather than work that actually demonstrates competency.

01
AI/ML Competency Depth
Does the artifact demonstrate a real, non-trivial AI/ML skill — not just tool usage? Can I explain the underlying model, algorithm, or ethical framework it applies? Weight: 30%
02
Real-World Applicability
Is this artifact grounded in a genuine business or organizational problem — not a toy dataset or classroom-only scenario? Does it produce an outcome a real organization could use? Weight: 25%
03
Audience Adaptability
Can this artifact be meaningfully re-framed for at least three different audiences without losing its integrity? Does it have both technical depth and explainable outcomes? Weight: 20%
04
Uniqueness to My Profile
Does this artifact reflect something only I could produce — combining my SAP domain expertise with AI/ML coursework in a way that a generic ML student could not replicate? Weight: 15%
05
Evidence of Process, Not Just Output
Does the artifact show how I think, not just what I produced? Is the methodology documented enough that a reviewer can evaluate my reasoning, not just my result? Weight: 10%

Step 3: Audience Adaptation Framework

The same artifact communicates different value to different audiences. Using the AI-Driven Bias Detection artifact as the primary example, here is how the framing, language, depth, and emphasis shift for each of the four target audiences.

🎯
SAP Hiring Managers & Recruiters
"Can this person do AI work on top of their SAP expertise?"
  • Lead with impact metrics: "80% manual reduction," "43 countries," "30% turnaround improvement"
  • Emphasize business outcomes over technical methodology
  • Use familiar SAP vocabulary: HCM, BTP, S/4HANA — establish credibility before introducing ML
  • Frame AI as an extension of existing SAP expertise, not a pivot away from it
  • Highlight tool names (Python, Scikit-learn) to pass ATS screening
🏗️
Enterprise Architects
"How does this fit into our existing SAP landscape and data governance?"
  • Lead with architecture diagrams — data flow, integration points, anonymization layer
  • Emphasize the infotype data model — show understanding of SAP data structures
  • Address data governance concerns: anonymization approach, no PII exposure
  • Explain the responsible AI checklist as an audit governance artifact
  • Discuss scalability: how the framework applies across HCM modules
🎓
Faculty & Academic Peers (AIML-500)
"Does this demonstrate rigorous understanding of fairness and ethical AI?"
  • Lead with fairness metric definitions — Demographic Parity, Equal Opportunity, Disparate Impact Ratio
  • Cite academic references — Barocas et al., IBM AIF360, EU AI Act
  • Show the tension between metrics — DP vs. EO can conflict; acknowledge the ambiguity
  • Emphasize the responsible AI framework as the primary intellectual contribution
  • Discuss limitations honestly: proxy variable problem, dataset constraints
🏛️
Organizations Seeking HR Automation
"Will AI in our HR system treat our employees fairly?"
  • Lead with the risk framing: "Automated HR systems can encode historical bias invisibly"
  • Avoid technical jargon — translate DIR as "the ratio of positive outcomes between groups"
  • Focus on the practical checklist — something their legal/compliance team can use
  • Show the before/after: what bias looks like without auditing vs. with
  • Emphasize regulatory relevance: EU AI Act, EEOC, employment law compliance

How I Applied This Framework

Step-by-Step Process
  1. Inventory all work: Listed every deliverable produced in AIML-500 and from professional experience — including work that felt "too technical" or "too SAP-specific" to include without adaptation.
  2. Define target audiences: Identified four distinct audiences for this portfolio — SAP hiring managers, enterprise architects, AIML-500 faculty/peers, and HR-automation-seeking organizations. Documented what each audience cares about and what success looks like for them.
  3. Build the evaluation matrix: Established five weighted criteria (AI/ML depth, real-world applicability, audience adaptability, uniqueness to my profile, evidence of process). Scored every candidate artifact against these criteria.
  4. Select and sequence: Used the weighted scores to select the final portfolio artifacts and determine their order — leading with the strongest AI/ML content (EthiRec) and the highest real-world impact work (Leave Approval Workflow) to establish credibility early.
  5. Adapt each artifact per audience: For each selected artifact, wrote four audience-specific framings covering different vocabulary, emphasis, depth, and value framing. The portfolio structure reflects the primary audience (SAP hiring managers) while the artifact detail pages serve deeper technical audiences.
  6. Validate through peer feedback: Incorporated Workshop 5 peer feedback to refine language, add missing context, and adjust emphasis — treating the peer review as a proxy for real audience response before publication.

Tools & Methods Used
  • Weighted scoring matrix — multi-criteria decision analysis applied to artifact selection
  • Audience persona mapping — four distinct audience profiles with explicit decision criteria
  • Value proposition design — artifact-specific VP for each piece, distinct from personal VP
  • Peer review feedback loop — Workshop 5 feedback used as audience proxy validation
  • Portfolio sequencing strategy — ordering artifacts to build credibility progressively
  • Language register adaptation — technical, business, academic, and executive framings
Key Learnings
  • The hardest curation decision was excluding technically strong work that lacked real-world grounding — a high ML score alone is not enough
  • Generic ML coursework exercises scored lowest despite being technically sound — because they could belong to any student
  • Audience adaptation is not "dumbing down" — it is precision targeting of what matters most to each reader
  • The evaluation matrix revealed that my SAP uniqueness is a competitive differentiator, not a liability, in an AI/ML portfolio
  • A portfolio without explicit audience mapping is a digital archive — it only becomes a resource site when the audience is named and the content is shaped around them
